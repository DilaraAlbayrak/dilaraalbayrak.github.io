Photometric Stereo for Embroidery Digitisation

An exploratory study developed as a proof of concept for the digitisation of textile cultural heritage, using photometric stereo to recover surface normals of embroidered textiles from synthetic captures generated in Unity under parametric spherical lighting.

Project Overview

This project investigates whether classical photometric stereo, combined with a controlled synthetic capture setup, can recover meaningful surface normals from embroidered textile samples. Embroidery presents a particular challenge for conventional flat scanning because its three-dimensional structure, including stitch height and thread weave, carries much of its visual and material character. The aim was to produce a lightweight pipeline that captures this surface detail in a form suitable for downstream visualisation, relighting, or further geometric processing.

Synthetic Capture in Unity A virtual lighting rig was constructed around the target textile sample, with point light sources distributed across a sphere. Light positions are parameterised in spherical coordinates along the roll axis (shown in red) and yaw axis (shown in yellow) in the diagram above. The user specifies the number of lights and their angular spacing, and the system enables only the configured subset of lights for each capture pass. The camera remains fixed, and a grayscale image is rendered for each lighting configuration, with the corresponding light direction encoded directly in the filename.

Photometric Stereo Solve Captured images and their associated light direction vectors are fed into a photometric stereo solver based on the Lambertian reflectance model. Surface normals are recovered per pixel via least-squares using the pseudo-inverse of the light direction matrix. An RGB variant runs the same solve on each colour channel independently, which can offer modest improvements on textiles with strong colour variation.

Output and Limitations The recovered normals are encoded as an RGB normal map, preserving fine-scale surface detail that is visually informative for embroidery characterisation. The implementation is intentionally exploratory: the Lambertian assumption breaks down on shiny or metallic threads, the synthetic capture setup does not model self-shadowing between stitches, and the results have not been validated against ground-truth geometry. Despite these constraints, the modular structure of the pipeline, from Unity capture through light parsing to solver, is straightforward to extend with more advanced reflectance models or to adapt for real-world physical captures.
